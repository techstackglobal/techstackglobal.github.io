Product Overview

The Samsung 990 PRO utilizes the PCIe 4.0 interface to push data transfer limits to the absolute maximum. It boasts sequential read speeds up to 7,450 MB/s and write speeds up to 6,900 MB/s. But raw speed isn't its only trick. Samsung overhauled their in-house controller to dramatically improve thermal efficiency, meaning the drive stays faster for much longer under heavy workloads compared to previous generations.

Alternative Budget Option

If you need extra storage but don't require absolute peak speeds, consider the Samsung 980 PRO. While the 990 PRO offers superior efficiency and random read/write speeds, the older 980 PRO still maxes out at a respectable 7,000 MB/s and can often be found at a discount. For basic remote work and casual gaming, the older drive provides incredible value.

However, if you are building a new machine from scratch or heavily upgrading a workstation (like the Dell XPS 15), we highly recommend securing the 990 PRO to future-proof your investment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the Samsung 990 PRO the best SSD for video editing?

Yes, with sequential read speeds up to 7,450 MB/s, it handles high-resolution 4K and 8K video timelines exceptionally well, eliminating the timeline stutter common with older SATA or slower NVMe drives.

How does the Samsung 990 PRO vs 980 PRO compare?

The 990 PRO offers up to 55% better random read/write performance and up to 50% better power efficiency compared to the older 980 PRO, making it significantly better for laptops and sustained heavy workloads.

Do I need the heatsink version?

If you are installing it in a desktop PC without a built-in motherboard heat shield, or inside a PlayStation 5, the heatsink version is highly recommended to prevent thermal throttling. Most laptops do not have room for the heatsink version.

Is 2TB enough storage for content creators?

For most freelance creators, 2TB is the optimal "sweet spot" for an active operating and scratch drive, allowing you to keep all your active, in-progress project files local before eventually archiving them to an external drive or NAS.
